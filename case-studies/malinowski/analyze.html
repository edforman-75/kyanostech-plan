How AI Gets Its Information

The Causality Chain

AI platforms don't generate information from nothing. They cite sources. Understanding where they get information reveals how to change what they say.

1

Campaign creates content

Website pages, press releases, social media posts, earned media coverage. This is what the campaign controls directly.

↓
2

Search engines index the content

Google crawls and indexes campaign website content. Well-structured pages with proper SEO and schema markup rank higher and get indexed faster.

↓
3

AI models retrieve and synthesize

When a voter asks a question, Google AI Overview pulls from indexed content. ChatGPT searches the web in real-time. Both prioritize authoritative, recent, consistent sources.

↓
4

Voter sees the response

The AI response is shaped by what sources exist and how prominent they are. If hostile sources are the only ones discussing a topic, that's what AI cites.

Why This Matters for Malinowski

The ethics controversy dominates AI responses because hostile sources are the only ones talking about the topic. The Campaign Legal Center complaint, Business Insider articles, and Wikipedia's controversy section are all indexed and authoritative.

The campaign website has no counter-narrative. No ethics FAQ page. No explanation of the blind trust. No mention of co-sponsoring the stock trading ban. When AI systems look for the campaign's perspective, they find nothing.

The Actionable Insight

Creating an ethics page on malinowskifornj.com creates an authoritative source that AI can cite. It doesn't remove hostile coverage—but it gives AI an alternative perspective to present alongside it.

What the Campaign Controls
High Control

Campaign Website

Page content, SEO titles, meta descriptions, schema markup, news/press section. Changes take effect immediately; Google re-indexes within days.

High Control

Press Strategy

Which stories to pitch, op-ed placements, earned media priorities. New coverage creates new sources for AI to cite.

Medium Control

Social Media Presence

Campaign accounts on Twitter/X, Facebook, Instagram. AI sometimes cites these, but less weight than news or official websites.

Medium Control

Endorsement Announcements

Joint releases with endorsers, press events. Creates coverage that AI can cite for "who supports" questions.

Low Control

Wikipedia

Can suggest edits but not control content. Major source for AI but requires neutral sourcing. Focus on creating sources Wikipedia can cite.

Low Control

Third-Party Coverage

News outlets, fact-checkers, political coverage. Campaign can pitch but not dictate. Hostile coverage persists.

The Social → AI → Voter Pipeline

Social media content can influence AI responses in two ways:

  1. Direct citation: AI sometimes cites YouTube videos, tweets, or viral social posts when answering questions.
  2. Press amplification: Journalists discover stories on social media, write articles, and those articles become AI sources.

The 256K attack ad views are not just reaching voters directly—they're creating a narrative that could influence press coverage and AI responses about the candidate.

The Priority Logic

Prioritize actions where the campaign has full control and impact is high. Website changes are immediate and certain. Press coverage is valuable but uncertain. Wikipedia can't be directly controlled.

The first three priorities can all be completed within 24-48 hours. They address the most critical gaps: the ethics narrative, the "not running" problem, and the stale content problem.

Once website changes are complete, shift focus to generating press coverage that reinforces the new narratives. The website provides the foundation that press coverage builds on.
