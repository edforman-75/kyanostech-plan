Third-Party Source

Wikipedia: What Campaigns Can (and Can't) Do

Wikipedia is a primary source for AI platforms. The article is missing key information but requires careful handling.

Why This Matters

Wikipedia's Tom Malinowski article is cited by Google AI Overview, ChatGPT, and the Knowledge Panel. It mentions the stock trading controversy but NOT the blind trust resolution or the STOCK Act reform co-sponsorship. These omissions hurt AI responses across all platforms.

What the Campaign Should NOT Do
  • Do not directly edit the Wikipedia article — Wikipedia has strict conflict-of-interest policies. Campaign staff editing the candidate's article violates these policies and edits will be reverted.
  • Do not pay anyone to edit — Paid editing without disclosure is against Wikipedia rules and can result in the article being locked.
What the Campaign CAN Do
  • Post on the Talk page — Go to the article's Talk page and request corrections with reliable sources. Example: "The article mentions stock trading issues but not the resolution. Here is a source: [link to news article about blind trust]"
  • Ensure press coverage is accurate — Wikipedia editors cite news articles. If press coverage mentions the blind trust and STOCK Act reform, Wikipedia editors may add it.
  • Alert supporters (not paid staff) — Volunteers and supporters who are not compensated by the campaign can make good-faith edits if they disclose any affiliation.
  • Provide source material — Create content on the campaign website that journalists can cite, which Wikipedia editors can then reference.
Key Information Missing from Wikipedia
  • Blind trust established to resolve ethics concerns
  • Co-sponsored the Ban Conflicted Trading Act (stock trading ban)
  • 2026 NJ-11 special election candidacy
  • Recent endorsements (Andy Kim, etc.)
